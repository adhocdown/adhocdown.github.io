{ Learning Curve }

One of the most appealing aspects of computer science is that it brings an unending stream of new and interesting problems. The umbrella of Extended Reality (XR), in particular, introduces a human component to this discipline. At the start of my career, I spent one summer working with Dr. Victoria Interrante at the University of Minnesota. There I was introduced to research at the intersections of virtual reality (VR), medicine, and perception. The potential for VR to unite seemingly disparate disciplines is boundless and speaks to its nascent potential. In Interrante's lab I conducted preliminary investigations into the realm of neurocognitive assessment via immersive simulation. The assessment was designed to assist diagnosis for patients with a realm of impairments, such as: dementia, alzheimer's, and traumatic brain injury.

At my alma mater, Rhodes College, I continued my research pursuits with Dr. Betsy Williams Sanders. For my senior thesis, I conducted an investigation into the effect of interpersonal familiarity on collaboration in virtual reality. In other words, I wanted to discover if interacting with another human prior to collaboration in VR would affect performance. During my time at Rhodes, I also pioneered a coding camp for middle and high school girls in the Memphis area alongside Dr. Sanders. Designing the curriculum for Camp Codette is easily one of my proudest achievements. I developed sessions that used Scratch, Lego Mindstorm, 3D printing, Python, and even Unity to inspire the curiosity of young students. The camp was largely funded by a charitable donation from Google Education's igniteCS program. I later participated on a panel at SIGCSE 2016 that discussed strategies to encourage undergraduate retention in computer science through outreach projects.

~ ~ ~ ~ ~ ~

Currently, I am working at the Learning in Virtual Environments Lab (LiVE) at Vanderbilt University in pursuit of my PhD. Under the guidance of Dr. Bobby Bodenheimer, I primarily pursue two avenues of research.

The first addresses navigation with and perceived affordances of holograms in augmented reality (AR). The second topic examines how cognitive development affects children's spatial reasoning in VR. However, I also conduct some work in the realm of visualization, especially for visual deficits and the ear.

On campus I also enjoy helping out Vanderbilt University's Women in Computing, which is an affiliated ACM-W student chapter. In 2017, a team of undergraduates and I hosted an emerging technology symposium with a number of inspiring speakers, including Christina Heller of VR Playhouse.

When I have the free time, I enjoy making graphite illustrations, surfing, and jogging around the neighborhood. Although, I have not been able to surf much since moving to Tennessee :)
