{ Learning Curve }

While I might have taken any number of paths, I feel fortunate to have stumbled onto the road to virtual reality. The interdisciplinary and novel nature of the beast, allows me to tap into my favorite skill sets. In the pursuit for knowledge, I am creative. I solve interesting problems. I get to spend long hours engrossed in projects that I enjoy. And, best of all, I am challenged every day.

One of the most appealing aspects of computer science is that it brings an unending stream of new and interesting problems. Virtual reality (VR), in particular, introduces a uniquely human component to this discipline. At the beginning of my career, I had the pleasure of working with Dr. Victoria Interrante at the University of Minnesota. There I was introduced to research at the intersections of computer science, medicine, perception, and just about everything else. The potential for VR to unite seemingly disparate disciplines is boundless and speaks to its nascent potential. In Interrante's lab I conducted preliminary investigations into the realm of neurocognitive assessment via immersive simulation. The assessment was designed to assist diagnosis for patients with a realm of impairments, such as: dementia, alzheimer's, and traumatic brain injury.

At my alma mater, Rhodes College, I continued my research pursuits with Dr. Betsy Williams Sanders. For my senior thesis, I conducted an investigation into the effect of interpersonal familiarity on collaboration in virtual reality. In other words, I wanted to discover if interacting with another human prior to collaboration in VR would affect performance. During my time at Rhodes, I also pioneered a coding camp for middle and high school girls in the Memphis area alongside Dr. Sanders. Designing the curriculum for Camp Codette is easily one of my proudest achievements. I developed sessions that used Scratch, Lego Mindstorm, 3D printing, Python, and even Unity to inspire the curiosity of young students. The camp was largely funded by a charitable donation from Google Education's igniteCS program. I later participated on a panel at SIGCSE 2016 that discussed strategies to encourage undergraduate retention in computer science through outreach projects.

Currently, I am working at the Learning in Virtual Environments Lab (LIVE) at Vanderbilt University in pursuit of my Ph.D. Under the guidance of Dr. Robert Bodenheimer, I am pursuing two avenues of research.

The first involves analyzing how cognitive development affects children's perception and action in VR. Children have largely been absent from VR literature due to the expensive and cumbersome nature of older VR technology. However, it is conceivable that stages of cognitive development may either enhance or hinder perception in immersive media. This would have dire implications for the design of future social, education, and training applications. My research seeks to isolate these differences and ascertain how they will affect children's interactions with VR.

The second topic I am pursuing is in the realm of visualization. Using patient data, I can graphically generate simulations to enhance training and understanding. I am currently in the process of developing both a visual deficit simulation and an interface for cochlear implant training. In the deficit simulation, I have mapped the user's visual field to the corresponding field of view of a head-mounted display, and I have generated a graphic representation of perturbations found in patient data to produce a deficit mapping. The cochlear implant training module is in its nascent stages, but I am excited to develop unique 3D interactions and controls.
